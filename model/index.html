01 — The Market

A $40B sport that still runs drinks on a slow-moving cart.

There are ~16,000 golf courses in the US and golfers played a record 545M+ rounds last year. About 80% of those rounds happen at public, daily-fee and municipal courses — exactly the courses we target, and exactly the ones with the weakest beverage tech.

02 — Economics of One Round

A foursome is a 4-hour captive bar tab.

Four golfers, ~4.5 hours, beer at $4–7 a can, and a cart that comes by maybe 3–4 times. More than half of golfers already buy something — and the ones who order on their phone spend more, more often.

The wedge: beverage carries the margin — bars run ~75% gross, beer is marked up 200–300%. Food is where courses actually lose money. So we lead with drinks (pure margin, simple logistics) and add food as a second act. The single biggest lever isn't price — it's the attach-rate gap: nearly half of golfers buy nothing, and the cart can't reach them.
03 — The Fee Model

The course pays nothing. That's the whole strategy.

The fastest way to 200 courses is to remove every reason to say no. No setup fee, no monthly SaaS, no contract to budget for. All revenue comes from the transaction itself — so onboarding a course is a 10-minute yes, not a procurement cycle. Here are the four structures, and the one we lead with.

Lead with this

C · Customer fee + course kickback

Course pays $0 — and we pay them
  • Golfer pays a small service fee ($1.99 + ~10%) + processing, like DoorDash.
  • We cut the course 5–10% of sales — "found money" on drinks they weren't selling.
  • Flips the pitch from "sign up & pay us" to "sign up & we send you a check."
  • Instant yes. Course has literal zero downside and new revenue.
Simplest

A · Pure customer fee

Course pays $0, keeps 100% of sales
  • Golfer covers the service fee + processing; course keeps every dollar of liquor revenue.
  • Cleanest possible pitch, highest take for us per order.
  • Mirrors Airstryk (free for course & golfer to install, golfer pays the fee).
  • No "we pay you" hook — slightly less magnetic than C in the sales call.
Situational

B · Built-in menu markup

Course pays $0, fee is invisible
  • 10–15% baked into drink prices instead of a visible add-on fee.
  • Golfer sees one clean price — no "junk fee" feeling.
  • Course must agree to a price bump; on-course prices are already high.
  • Harder to show the course a transparent payout. Use where fee-aversion is high.
Enterprise only

D · Course-paid SaaS

Course pays $149–499/mo
  • The original model — subscription + tiny per-order fee, lower/zero golfer fee.
  • Best margin if they'll pay, and the model our deck already documents.
  • Adds onboarding friction — a budget line & a decision-maker. Kills speed.
  • Keep only for white-label / multi-course operator deals (20 logos in one sale).
Recommendation: Lead with C to land courses fast (the kickback is the magnet), keep A as the default where a course doesn't care about the kickback, and reserve D for enterprise white-label. One decision to tune: how generous the kickback is — modeled at 5% below, every point costs us ~$0.18/order but buys goodwill and exclusivity.
04 — Unit Economics

What we make on one order, and one course.

Base case, Option C. An "order" = a foursome's round of drinks, $18 average. Processing is passed to the golfer (industry-standard), so our take is the service fee minus the course kickback.

⚙️ Why courses scale fast

Zero-friction onboarding + a kickback means the bottleneck is reps and runners, not contracts. Each course is a ~10-min yes and near-zero marginal cost to us.

📊 Sensitivity

The swing is driven first by attach rate (8%→25% is a 4–5× revenue move), then AOV. Both rise with better signage, push offers, and food. Conservative still clears $1.2M at 200.

Honest caveats: these are mature, full-season run-rates — real year-one revenue is lower because courses sign mid-season and ramp adoption over time, and golf is seasonal (summer ≈ half of all rounds). Lift figures (+27% / +54%) are vendor-published; the model does not rely on them — it's built on the conservative attach-rate floor. Next step is a live pilot to replace assumed attach with a real number.
06 — Food vs. Beverage

Same fee, different kitchen.

Food rides the exact same fee structure — but the operations differ in one key way: drinks come off the roving cart, food comes out of the clubhouse kitchen. That changes dispatch and timing, not economics.

⚙️ The one framework difference: food needs a prep-time ETA, kitchen-capacity throttle, and a "meet at the turn" pickup option baked into the app — the kitchen can't fire 30 burgers at once the way a cart can hand out 30 beers. Same fee, same payout logic; we just add a kitchen-aware dispatch layer. Beverage launches first (margin-rich, zero prep); food turns on once a course is live and comfortable.
